Grace Hopper:

Pioneering the Future of Computing

"The most dangerous phrase in the language is, 'We've always done it this way.'"

Grace Brewster Hopper (née Murray; December 9, 1906 – January 1, 1992) was an American computer scientist, mathematician, and United States Navy rear admiral. A pioneer of computer programming, she was the first to propose the theory of machine-independent programming languages, leading to the development of the FLOW-MATIC language and COBOL. Hopper began her computing career in 1944 as part of the Harvard Mark I team, later contributing to the development of the UNIVAC I computer and managing the creation of early COBOL compilers. She advocated for simplified programming with an English-based syntax and played a significant role in the CODASYL consortium, which created COBOL. Hopper's impact on computing was profound, earning her numerous honors, including the Presidential Medal of Freedom in 2016. Her legacy includes the USS Hopper destroyer, the Cray XE6 supercomputer "Hopper," and the Nvidia "Grace Hopper" superchip.

Early Life

Grace Brewster Murray was born in New York City, the eldest of three children to Walter Fletcher Murray and Mary Campbell Van Horne. Her parents, of Scottish and Dutch descent, were active members of the West End Collegiate Church. Grace displayed a lifelong curiosity from a young age. At seven, she began dismantling alarm clocks to understand their workings, a habit that showcased her early penchant for understanding technology. Later in life, she kept a backward-running clock to challenge conventional thinking, stating, "Humans are allergic to change."

Education

For her education, Grace attended the Hartridge School in Plainfield, New Jersey, and later applied to Vassar College at the age of 16. Initially rejected due to low Latin scores, she was admitted the following year. In 1928, she graduated Phi Beta Kappa from Vassar with a bachelor's degree in mathematics and physics. Grace continued her academic journey at Yale University, earning her master's degree in 1930 and completing her Ph.D. in mathematics in 1934. Her dissertation, "New Types of Irreducibility Criteria," marked the beginning of her significant contributions to the field. She joined the faculty at Vassar in 1931, where she eventually became an associate professor by 1941, laying the groundwork for her groundbreaking work in computer science and programming later in life.

Career

Grace Hopper's career was shaped by her determination and pioneering spirit, beginning with her entry into the United States Navy Reserve during World War II. Despite initial setbacks, including age and weight requirements, Hopper excelled at the Naval Reserve Midshipmen's School and graduated top of her class in 1944. Assigned to the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard University, she collaborated closely with Howard H. Aiken on the Mark I computer, where they co-authored influential papers that laid the foundation for modern computing. Hopper's early work on the Mark I marked her as a trailblazer in the emerging field of computer science, showcasing her ability to bridge theoretical mathematics with practical applications.

Post-war, Hopper joined the Eckert–Mauchly Computer Corporation in 1949, becoming a senior mathematician. Her visionary ideas led her to advocate for a programming language based on English words, which eventually materialized in the form of COBOL. Despite initial skepticism, she persevered, developing the A-0 compiler and later playing a key role in the development of COBOL through her work with the CODASYL committee in 1959. Her dedication to creating a more user-friendly programming language revolutionized data processing, making it accessible to a wider range of users beyond mathematicians and engineers.

Appointed as the director of automatic programming at Eckert–Mauchly in 1954, Hopper oversaw the development of early programming languages like MATH-MATIC and FLOW-MATIC. Her leadership and contributions to COBOL revolutionized data processing, making programming more accessible and paving the way for widespread adoption in business applications. Throughout her career, Hopper continued to push for computing standards and advancements in networked computing, leaving an enduring legacy in the field of computer science.

Retirement

Grace Hopper's post-military career was as illustrious as her service. Retiring from the Naval Reserve as a commander in 1966, she was recalled to active duty in 1967 and again in 1972, where she eventually attained the rank of captain in 1973 under Admiral Elmo R. Zumwalt Jr. Her exceptional contributions were further recognized when, in December 1983, President Ronald Reagan appointed her to the rank of commodore on the retired list, later redesignated as rear admiral (lower half). Hopper continued to serve beyond the usual retirement age, receiving special approval from Congress until her final retirement on August 14, 1986.

Her retirement ceremony, held aboard the USS Constitution in Boston, marked a remarkable milestone: she was the oldest serving member of the Navy at 79 years, eight months, and five days. At this event, she was honored with the Defense Distinguished Service Medal, the highest non-combat award from the Department of Defense. Admiral Hopper's legacy extends beyond.

Post Retirement

Following her retirement from the Navy, Grace Hopper joined Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) as a senior consultant. Initially offered a position by Rita Yavinsky, she insisted on a formal interview process. In a jest, she proposed a position available on alternating Thursdays at their computing museum in exchange for a generous salary and unlimited expense account. Instead, she was hired as a full-time Principal Corporate Consulting Engineer, equivalent to a senior vice president. In this role, Hopper represented DEC at industry forums and served on various committees.

She remained with DEC until her passing at age 85 in 1992, primarily serving as a goodwill ambassador. Despite no longer being in the military, she wore her Navy uniform to lectures, receiving standing ovations at Digital's engineering facilities. In 2016, she was posthumously awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom for her profound contributions to computer science and her dedication to mentoring young talent, which she considered one of her greatest achievements alongside pioneering the compiler.

Awards
  • Presidential Medal of Freedom (2016): Awarded posthumously by President Barack Obama, the highest civilian honor in the United States.
  • Defense Distinguished Service Medal: Awarded during her retirement from the U.S. Navy in 1986, the highest non-combat decoration by the Department of Defense.
  • National Medal of Technology (1991): Received for her significant contributions to the development of computer programming languages.
  • Computer History Museum Fellow Award (1991): Recognized as a Fellow of the Computer History Museum for her seminal work in computer science.
  • Yale University Wilbur Lucius Cross Medal (1970): Honored for outstanding professional achievement and service to the nation.
  • Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) Distinguished Service Award (1973): Awarded for her service to the computing community.
  • American Association of University Women Achievement Award (1964): Recognized for her outstanding contributions to the field of computer science and technology.
